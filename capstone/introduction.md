## Introduction

The numerical approximation of compressible and viscous flow models remains a central challenge in modern scientific computing, particularly when simulations must resolve multiscale structures, sharp gradients, and under-resolved transient features on complex meshes. In such regimes, classical high-order methods can deliver excellent accuracy in smooth regions yet become fragile in the presence of discontinuities, strong nonlinear coupling, or geometric distortion. Recent work in numerical analysis has therefore emphasized structure-preserving design principles that balance approximation quality with nonlinear robustness. Among the most influential of these principles are entropy stability, positivity preservation of thermodynamic variables, and reformulation strategies that recast parabolic components into first-order hyperbolic systems. Taken together, these developments suggest that robust high-order simulation is less a matter of isolated flux choices and more a matter of coherent analytical design across interior operators, boundary/interface treatments, and time evolution.

Entropy stability has emerged as a rigorous organizing framework for high-order discretization of nonlinear conservation laws and compressible flow equations. The finite-difference constructions of Fisher and Carpenter provide a foundational demonstration that high-order accurate methods can be made entropy stable on finite domains through summation-by-parts-compatible operator design and carefully coupled numerical fluxes (Fisher and Carpenter, 2013). Parallel developments in entropy-stable collocation and spectral-element formulations show that similar principles extend to high-order polynomial approximation of the compressible Navier-Stokes equations, including discontinuous interfaces and companion dissipation mechanisms for shock handling (Carpenter et al., 2014). More recent discontinuous Galerkin spectral-element work on curvilinear hybrid meshes further indicates that entropy-stable formulations can be pushed toward the geometric complexity required in realistic applications (Keim et al., 2026). In this line of research, entropy inequalities are not merely post hoc diagnostics; they guide algorithm construction by constraining how discrete operators exchange information and produce dissipation.

At the same time, positivity-preserving mechanisms have become indispensable in practical high-order compressible flow solvers. Entropy-stable discretizations can control nonlinear growth in integral norms, but numerical failure can still occur if pointwise states leave the physically admissible set, for example through negative density, pressure, or temperature in extreme transients. This motivates methods that couple entropy-stable flux construction with explicit positivity control. The first-order positivity-preserving entropy-stable scheme of Upperman and Yamaleev provides a concrete example of this coupling in the 1-D compressible Navier-Stokes setting, and highlights the broader necessity of combining thermodynamic admissibility with entropy analysis in operational algorithms (Upperman and Yamaleev, 2022). From a theoretical perspective, the high-order entropy-stable program represented in part by Fjordholm's work also reinforces that entropy consistency and admissibility constraints must be developed jointly rather than sequentially if one seeks robust methods at scale (Fjordholm, 2013).

Another major thread in the literature concerns first-order hyperbolic reformulations of diffusion and viscous operators. Rather than directly discretizing second-order parabolic terms, this approach introduces auxiliary gradient-like variables and rewrites the governing equations as enlarged first-order systems. Nishikawa's two-part diffusion-program demonstrates that such reformulations can unify advection and diffusion treatment, yield rapidly convergent steady computations, and produce derivative quantities with accuracy comparable to that of primary variables on irregular grids (Nishikawa, 2007; Nishikawa, 2010). Closely related hyperbolic Navier-Stokes formulations extend this philosophy to compressible viscous flow, with three-dimensional and adaptive space-time solver studies reporting practical benefits in viscous discretization and derivative quality (Nakashima et al., 2016; Padway and Nishikawa, 2022). These results support a broader numerical-analysis viewpoint: hyperbolization can simplify algorithmic structure by allowing advection-like tools, including upwind logic, to act on a unified first-order system.

The diffusion-equation literature also provides an important complementary perspective through mimetic discretization. Mimetic finite difference methods emphasize exact preservation of discrete analogues of conservation laws and vector-calculus identities on nonorthogonal and unstructured grids, thereby improving consistency between discrete and continuous operators in heterogeneous media (Hyman et al., 2002). Although this body of work is not identical in formulation to entropy-stable SBP/DG frameworks, it addresses a mathematically adjacent objective: preserving structural identities that govern stability and conservation in the underlying PDE model. For contemporary high-order methods, this connection is significant. It suggests that entropy stability, positivity control, and mimetic compatibility should be viewed as interacting constraints in a single design space, not as independent features bolted onto a baseline solver.

Despite substantial progress, the current literature identifies several persistent limitations. First, entropy-stable interior operators are significantly more mature than entropy-stable physical boundary conditions for viscous compressible systems, where nonlinear stability, physical fidelity, and implementation tractability must be satisfied simultaneously (Carpenter et al., 2014; Fisher and Carpenter, 2013). Second, many stability guarantees remain semi-discrete; fully discrete methods that preserve entropy and positivity under practical time integration constraints are still an active frontier (Upperman and Yamaleev, 2022; Keim et al., 2026). Third, shock-capturing remains a delicate balance problem: dissipative companions are needed for robustness, but excessive dissipation can erode high-order accuracy and obscure the benefits of structure-preserving formulations (Carpenter et al., 2014; Fisher and Carpenter, 2013). Fourth, while first-order hyperbolic reformulations are promising, their integration with entropy-stable and positivity-preserving frameworks for complex three-dimensional and turbulent regimes remains incomplete (Nakashima et al., 2016; Padway and Nishikawa, 2022).

These limitations define a clear research gap at the intersection of theory and algorithm design. The field now has strong ingredients: entropy-stable high-order finite differences, entropy-stable collocation/DG constructions on increasingly complex meshes, positivity-preserving corrections, first-order diffusion/hyperbolic reformulations, and mimetic ideas for discrete compatibility. What is still missing is a unified high-order methodology that combines these ingredients with consistent guarantees across interior and boundary operators, discrete time advancement, and physically admissible state evolution. In other words, contemporary methods often excel along one or two axes, but a broadly deployable framework must succeed simultaneously on entropy stability, positivity, geometric flexibility, and viscous-hyperbolic coupling.

Motivated by this gap, a natural direction is to develop integrated formulations that treat entropy production, admissibility preservation, and first-order reformulation variables as co-designed components rather than afterthought constraints. Such a direction is aligned with the open problems identified in the current synthesis literature: entropy-stable boundary closures for viscous flows, fully discrete entropy-positivity compatibility, and robust companion dissipation strategies on unstructured high-order meshes. It is also aligned with the methodological trajectory from finite-difference SBP frameworks to hybrid-mesh DGSE formulations and hyperbolic Navier-Stokes solvers, all of which point toward unified, structure-aware discretization pipelines (Fisher and Carpenter, 2013; Carpenter et al., 2014; Keim et al., 2026; Nishikawa, 2010; Nakashima et al., 2016).

In this paper, we develop a unified high-order framework that couples entropy-stable discretization, positivity-preserving constraints, and first-order hyperbolic reformulations for diffusion and viscous compressible flow, with emphasis on boundary-stable closures and fully discrete robustness on complex meshes.
