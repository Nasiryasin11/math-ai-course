## Introduction

High-fidelity simulation of compressible and viscous flows requires numerical methods that are simultaneously high-order accurate, robust in the presence of nonlinear features, and computationally efficient on realistic meshes. These requirements are often in tension: methods designed for sharp resolution can lose nonlinear stability, while strongly stabilized schemes may sacrifice accuracy or increase algorithmic complexity. In this context, entropy-stable high-order discretizations and hyperbolic reformulations of diffusive systems have emerged as complementary lines of research that directly address stability, accuracy, and efficiency in a unified mathematical framework.

The entropy-stability perspective has become especially influential for nonlinear conservation laws and the compressible Navier-Stokes equations. By constructing discrete operators that mimic continuous entropy inequalities, one can obtain schemes with provable nonlinear stability properties while preserving high-order consistency. For finite-difference settings, high-order entropy-stable constructions on bounded domains demonstrate that discrete summation-by-parts structure, together with suitable interface and boundary coupling, can control nonlinear growth mechanisms without reverting to low-order dissipation (Fisher and Carpenter, 2013). For element-based formulations, entropy-stable spectral collocation methods extend these principles to high-order polynomial approximations and discontinuous interfaces, enabling stable high-order discretization of viscous compressible flow with strong geometric flexibility (Carpenter et al., 2014). Together, these developments show that entropy analysis is not only a diagnostic tool but also a constructive design principle for modern high-order solvers.

In parallel, first-order system reformulations provide an alternative route to robust discretization of equations with diffusion. Instead of treating second-order operators directly, this approach introduces auxiliary variables so that gradients and fluxes are evolved as part of an enlarged first-order system. Early work for diffusion equations shows that this reformulation supports residual-distribution discretizations and yields accurate solutions within a hyperbolic-like framework (Nishikawa, 2007). The framework is then generalized to advection-diffusion problems, where advection and diffusion are handled in a single first-order formulation, reducing methodological fragmentation between convective and viscous terms (Nishikawa, 2010). Beyond conceptual unification, this strategy can improve algorithmic structure by making upwind-oriented machinery applicable to a broader PDE class and by enabling coherent treatment of primary and derivative variables.

These two strands naturally converge in hyperbolic Navier-Stokes methods, which recast viscous flow systems into first-order forms suitable for robust, transport-oriented solvers. Hyperbolic Navier-Stokes solvers for three-dimensional flows indicate that such formulations can be practically implemented for complex geometries while retaining competitive robustness for viscous compressible problems (Nakashima et al., 2016). When viewed alongside entropy-stable discretization theory, this suggests a promising synthesis: reformulate for algorithmic and structural advantages, then enforce discrete entropy principles to secure nonlinear stability at high order.

Despite this progress, several foundational issues remain open and motivate further work. First, entropy-stable treatment of physical boundaries is still substantially harder than entropy-stable interior discretization, particularly for viscous Navier-Stokes systems where boundary data must satisfy both physical and thermodynamic constraints (Carpenter et al., 2014). Second, much of the current theory is semi-discrete, so extending guarantees to fully discrete schemes requires time integrators that preserve entropy stability and positivity properties in practice. Additional unresolved directions include the design of shock-capturing companion operators that preserve high-order accuracy near discontinuities and the extension of these methods to turbulence and other complex physics regimes.

Accordingly, current research is moving toward integrated high-order frameworks that couple entropy-compatible operators, robust interface and boundary closures, and first-order hyperbolic reformulations for diffusive and viscous terms. Such integration is mathematically appealing because it aligns continuous thermodynamic structure with discrete approximation design, and it is computationally appealing because it can support scalable, high-resolution simulation across mixed-flow regimes where advection, diffusion, and nonlinear wave interactions coexist.

In this paper, we develop and analyze an entropy-stable high-order framework that combines finite-difference and spectral-collocation design principles with first-order hyperbolic reformulations for diffusion and viscous flow, with emphasis on boundary-stable closures and fully discrete robustness.
